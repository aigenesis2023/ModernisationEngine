The Five Levels of Hazard Control

01. Elimination expand_more
Physically remove the hazard. This is the most effective control measure. If the hazard is not there, the risk is zero.
02. Substitution expand_more
Replace the hazard with something less dangerous. For example, replacing a toxic chemical with a non-toxic alternative.
03. Engineering Controls expand_more
Isolate people from the hazard. This involves physical changes to the workplace, like machine guards or ventilation systems.
04. Administrative Controls expand_more
Change the way people work. Includes training, signs, and procedures that manage how the exposure occurs.
05. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) expand_more
Protect the worker with equipment. This is the last line of defense and relies on correct usage and fit.

Glossary of Terms

Hazard

A potential source of harm or adverse health effect on a person or persons.

Risk

The likelihood that a person may be harmed or suffer adverse health effects if exposed to a hazard.

Near Miss

An unplanned event that did not result in injury, illness, or damage—but had the potential to do so.

PPE

Personal Protective Equipment designed to minimize exposure to hazards that cause serious injuries.

SDS

Safety Data Sheet. Document that provides health and safety information about products and chemicals.

Incident

An occurrence, condition, or situation arising in the course of work that resulted in or could have resulted in injuries.

01. Setting

Busy Distribution Hub

02. Incident

The Forklift Tip

A driver attempted a sharp turn to avoid a pallet, leading to a lateral tip-over. Fortunately, the overhead guard prevented injury.

Beyond Speed
