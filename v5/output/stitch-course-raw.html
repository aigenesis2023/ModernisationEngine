Hierarchy of Controls

The gold standard for workplace safety prioritization.

This systemic approach ensures that the most effective methods—removing the hazard entirely—are considered before relying on human behavior or personal protective equipment.

01 The Five Levels of Hazard Control

1. Elimination expand_more
Physically remove the hazard. This is the most effective control measure. Example: Removing a trip hazard from a walkway.
2. Substitution expand_more
Replace the hazard with a safer alternative. Example: Using a water-based solvent instead of a flammable chemical.
3. Engineering Controls expand_more
Isolate people from the hazard through physical barriers or mechanical systems. Example: Installing machine guarding or ventilation.
4. Administrative Controls expand_more
Change the way people work through training, signage, and policy. Example: Safety training sessions or warning signs.
5. PPE (Personal Protective Equipment) expand_more
Protect the worker with equipment. This is the least effective control as it relies on human compliance and equipment failure is possible.

Physical Hazards

Physical hazards are environmental factors that can harm an employee without necessarily touching them. These include heights, noisy environments, radiation, and extreme temperatures.
