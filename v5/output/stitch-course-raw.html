Understanding the Hierarchy of Controls

The Hierarchy of Controls is a systematic approach to eliminating or reducing workplace hazards. It prioritizes methods that are most effective at protecting workers, moving from the most reliable physical changes to the least reliable administrative and personal protective measures.

lightbulb

Always aim for Elimination before settling for Personal Protective Equipment (PPE).

The Five Levels of Hazard Control

1. Elimination expand_more
Physically remove the hazard. This is the most effective control measure. If the task or material isn't necessary, remove it entirely.
2. Substitution expand_more
Replace the hazard with something less dangerous. For example, using water-based paint instead of solvent-based paint.
3. Engineering Controls expand_more
Isolate people from the hazard. Installing physical barriers, ventilation systems, or machine guarding.
4. Administrative Controls expand_more
Change the way people work. Training, procedure changes, and signage are examples of administrative controls.
5. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) expand_more
Protect the worker with equipment. Hard hats, gloves, and earplugs. This is the last line of defense.

Emergency Response Sequence

1

Immediate Alert

Trigger the manual fire alarm and announce the emergency clearly to others in the vicinity.

2

Emergency Services Contact

Call 911 (or local equivalent). Provide precise location, nature of emergency, and number of affected people.

3

Controlled Evacuation

Evacuate via the nearest safe exit. Do not use elevators. Assist those with mobility challenges.

Essential Safety Vocabulary

Hazard

Anything with the potential to cause harm, ill health, or damage to property.

Risk

The likelihood that a hazard will cause harm, combined with the severity of that harm.

Near Miss

An unplanned event that did not result in injury or damage but had the potential to do so.
