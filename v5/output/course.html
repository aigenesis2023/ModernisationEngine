Recognising an EV

Why Recognition Matters

It is important that you can recognise an electric vehicle when it comes into your workplace. You will then be ready to exercise the correct procedures necessary for a safe visit.

Identifying features of electric vehicles differ between manufacturers. You may need to exercise your skill and experience to make sure you know what type of vehicle you are looking at — getting it wrong could be risky.

Exterior Identification Features

Number Plate expand_more

Fully electric vehicles may have a green border on their number plate, though this is not mandatory.

Badging expand_more

Many EVs will have a badge indicating that they are an electric vehicle. Look for manufacturer-specific EV emblems.

Charge Port expand_more

On Battery Electric Vehicles (BEV) and Plug-in Hybrids (PHEV) there will be a battery charge lead connector port somewhere on the vehicle.

Orange High Voltage Cables expand_more

Bright-coloured (typically orange) high voltage cables are a clear indicator of an electric or hybrid vehicle.

Warning Labels expand_more

Look for high voltage warning labels, particularly in the engine bay and near battery components.

Instrument Cluster expand_more

EV-specific warnings, messages, and power flow/energy use screens on the dashboard indicate an electric drivetrain.

Interior Clues

You can also identify an EV by key features of the interior. Look for the power flow/energy use screen, absence of a traditional gear selector, and the instrument cluster showing battery charge level rather than a fuel gauge.

The power controller/inverter and high voltage warning labels under the bonnet are additional confirmation.

Not all types of EV share the same identifying features. Use this reference table to understand which identifiers apply to each vehicle type.

Types of Electric Vehicle

Exploring Vehicle Types

Understanding the differences between vehicle types is essential for safe working practices.

Vehicle Categories

ICE

Internal Combustion Engine

Powered solely by a petrol or diesel engine — no electric motor or battery for propulsion. Energy is produced by burning fuel and transmitted mechanically to the wheels. Emits CO₂ and other pollutants. Still common globally, but being phased out in many regions due to environmental regulations.

BEV

Battery Electric Vehicle

Driven entirely by an electric motor, with no internal combustion engine. Has a large capacity high voltage battery to provide the required driving range. Instant torque gives strong performance with no engine noise. The high voltage system can be identified by orange or brightly coloured cabling.

HEV

Hybrid Electric Vehicle

Usually fitted with an ICE, high voltage battery, and electric motor assembly. Some use their electric motor simultaneously with the ICE to drive the wheels (Parallel Hybrid). Others use the petrol engine to charge the battery only (Series Hybrid). Some can switch between both modes (Series/Parallel Hybrid).

PHEV

Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicle

The high voltage battery can be charged using an external mains charger. Has a larger capacity battery than standard hybrids, increasing electric-only range. The vehicle also has a combustion engine for extended range. Can operate as Parallel or Series hybrid depending on the model.

MHEV

Mild Hybrid Electric Vehicle

Uses a combustion engine with a belt-integrated starter generator that provides extra power when pulling away or accelerating. Cannot operate in fully electric mode. Typically operates at 48V — below the recognised definition of "High Voltage". The 48V system wiring is often coloured purple or blue.

The EV Family Tree

Electric vehicles form a family tree of related technologies. At the top level, vehicles split into ICE, Hybrid, Battery Electric, and Fuel Cell Electric categories.

Hybrids further divide into Mild Hybrid, Parallel Hybrid, Series Hybrid, Series/Parallel Hybrid, and Plug-in Hybrid variants. Each has different safety implications for workshop staff.

Under the Bonnet

format_quote
In the context of Electric Vehicles, any voltage greater than 60 volts is classified as a high voltage system.

Key EV Components

01 / 06

HV Battery Pack

High voltage traction battery — typically 60–800V. Powers the electric drive motors. This is the primary energy storage component and represents the most significant safety consideration.

02 / 06

Power Electronics Controller

Contains the inverter to change AC to DC current and vice versa, plus the electronic controller that manages power flow throughout the vehicle.

03 / 06

Electric Drive Motor

Powered by high voltage AC from the electronics controller to drive the vehicle. In some configurations, it can also work as a generator during regenerative braking.

04 / 06

DC/DC Converter

Converts high voltage from the traction battery to low voltage (12V) for the vehicle's conventional electrical systems — lights, radio, and control modules.

05 / 06

Battery Cooling System

Maintains optimal battery temperature. Essential for performance, longevity, and safety. Overheating batteries can lead to thermal runaway.

06 / 06

On-Board Charger

Found in BEVs and PHEVs. Takes mains electricity and converts it to charge the vehicle's high voltage battery. Connected via the mains charging port.

Safety & Hazards

High Voltage Hazards

The UK Health and Safety Executive warns that voltages present in electric and hybrid vehicles are significantly higher than conventional vehicles — currently up to 650V DC compared to 12/24V DC in traditional vehicles.

In dry conditions, accidental contact with parts that are live at voltages above 110V DC can be fatal.

Battery systems may contain chemicals that can be harmful if released. They also store significant amounts of energy that can give rise to explosion if not dealt with correctly.

Workplace Risks

bolt

Electric Shock

High voltage components and cabling capable of delivering a fatal electric shock. Stored electrical energy can cause arc/flash.

speed

Silent Operation

People may be unaware of vehicles moving as they are silent when electrically driven. AVAS warning systems may not sound as expected.

shield

Battery Hazards

Potential release of explosive gases and harmful liquids if batteries are damaged or incorrectly modified.

memory

Residual Voltage

Components may retain dangerous voltage even when the vehicle is switched off.

hub

Unexpected Movement

Electric motors or the vehicle itself may move unexpectedly due to magnetic forces within the motors.

star

Medical Devices

The electrical systems on the vehicle may affect medical devices such as pacemakers.

ECE R100 — What Counts as High Voltage?

ECE R100 defines "High Voltage" as an electric component or circuit with working voltage between >60V and ≤1500V DC or >30V and ≤1000V AC (root mean square).

These values are used specifically when referring to Electric Vehicles. Please note that high voltage is defined differently in other industry sectors.

Emergency Response

Electric Shock Effects

info
Heart

A voltage as low as 50V can block electrical signals between the brain and muscles, potentially stopping the heart from beating properly.

local_fire_department
Breathing

Electric shock can prevent the person from breathing by disrupting the nerve signals that control respiratory muscles.

pan_tool
Muscles

Electrical current passing through the body causes severe muscle spasms that can prevent the victim from releasing the source.

bolt
Secondary Injuries

Burns at entry and exit points, fall injuries from being thrown or losing balance, and internal tissue damage are common secondary effects.

Electric Shock Response Steps

01. Isolate the Power

For mains systems (vehicle chargers for BEV/PHEV), turn off the power at the isolation switch. For high voltage vehicles, the supply is normally disconnected when turning the vehicle off — but consider there may be a fault.

02. Do Not Touch the Casualty

Do not touch the casualty if they are still in contact with the electrical source — you are at risk of electrocution.

03. Use a Rescue Hook

If safe to do so, use a purpose-designed insulated rescue hook to separate them from the power supply. The rescue hook should be kept visible and accessible at the EV charging bay at all times.

04. Call 999

Call emergency services immediately for professional medical assistance.

05. Perform Primary Survey

Once contact is broken, perform a primary survey and treat injuries. Seek assistance from the company first aid person if available.

06. Secure the Area

Cordon off the vehicle using a non-conductive safety barrier chain. No one should work on the vehicle until it has been made safe and the risk reassessed.

Charging

Charging Your Knowledge

Understanding charging terminology and systems is essential for safe EV handling.

Charging Terminology

Voltage (Volts)

The term used to describe electrical pressure. ICE vehicles use 12V, trucks 24V, mild hybrids 48V, hybrids 200–400V typically, and full EVs 350–800V typically.

Amperage (Amps)

The measurement used to work out electrical flow. A higher amp reading indicates a larger current is flowing. There is a direct relationship between voltage, amps, and circuit resistance.

Power (Watts/kW)

Refers to energy consumption. Home charging typically delivers 2.3kW. Wall boxes deliver 7.4–22kW. Rapid chargers are 50kW+ with the fastest in the UK reaching 360kW.
