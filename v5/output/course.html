Why Recognition Matters

It is important that you can recognise an electric vehicle when it comes into your workplace. You will then be ready to exercise the correct procedures necessary for a safe visit.

Identifying features of electric vehicles will differ between manufacturers. This means that you may need to exercise your skill and experience to make sure that you know what type of vehicle you are looking at — getting it wrong could be risky.

Understanding the EV Family

Electric vehicles come in many forms — from mild hybrids to fully electric and hydrogen fuel cell. Each has different components, capabilities, and safety considerations.

EV Types Compared

BEV — Battery Electric

Driven 100% by electric motor with a large capacity rechargeable high voltage battery.

Advantages: No tailpipe emissions, greatest electric-only driving range of all EV types. Easy to drive.

Disadvantages: Reliance on charging network, battery repair costs compared to ICE repair costs.

HEV — Hybrid Electric

Internal combustion engine combined with electric motor and smaller high voltage battery capacity.

Advantages: Good compromise between range and clean running. Optimised use of both powertrains can lead to very high MPG figures.

Disadvantages: Extra cost of EV systems. Battery-only range will be less than a BEV due to smaller battery sizes.

PHEV — Plug-in Hybrid

High voltage battery can be charged by an external mains charger, hence the "Plug-in" hybrid name.

Advantages: Offers both range and clean running. Larger battery than non plug-in hybrids. Can be charged from the mains.

Disadvantages: Cost of EV systems means these vehicles are more expensive. Battery-only range will still be less than a BEV.

EREV — Extended-Range

100% electric motor drive. An external fuel-based power unit drives a generator to charge the battery and extend the vehicle range.

Advantages: Combines electric drive with extended range from a fuel-powered generator.

Disadvantages: Stretches the definition of "Hybrid". Complex dual powertrain.

FCEV — Fuel Cell Electric

Driven 100% by electric motor. Fuel cell converts hydrogen and oxygen into electric power.

Advantages: Very clean running. Long range. Zero tailpipe emissions, producing only clean water vapour.

Disadvantages: Expensive and rare. Hydrogen fueling infrastructure is not widely available.

ICE — Conventional

Traditional internal combustion engine running on petrol or diesel.

Advantages: No cost of batteries or EV HV control systems. Good range. Quick to refuel.

Disadvantages: Tailpipe emissions, noise pollution. Being phased out in various countries.

Hazards Around High Voltage Electrical Systems

Understanding the dangers associated with electric and hybrid vehicles is critical to keeping yourself and your colleagues safe.

The Core Mandate

HSE Legislation

The UK Health and Safety Executive states that people in the motor vehicle repair and recovery industry need to be aware of the additional hazards when working with Electric & Hybrid Vehicles.

Key fact: Voltages in E&HVs reach up to 650 Volts DC — far higher than the 12/24V in conventional vehicles. In dry conditions, contact with parts live at voltages above 110V DC can be fatal.

Battery systems may contain harmful chemicals and store significant energy that can cause explosion if not handled correctly.

110V

Workplace Risks from Electric & Hybrid Vehicles

Electric Shock & Arc Flash expand_more

High voltage components and cabling capable of delivering a fatal electric shock. Stored electrical energy can cause explosion, fire, and arc/flash.

Residual Voltage expand_more

Components may retain a dangerous voltage even when the vehicle is switched off.

Unexpected Movement expand_more

Electric motors or the vehicle itself may move unexpectedly due to magnetic forces within the motors.

Silent Operation expand_more

People may be unaware of vehicles moving as EVs are silent in operation.

Battery Hazards expand_more

Potential for release of explosive gases and harmful chemicals if batteries are damaged. Manual handling risks with battery replacement.

Medical Device Interference expand_more

Vehicle electrical systems may affect medical devices such as pacemakers.

Emergency Response — Electric Shock

01. Isolate the Power

For mains systems (chargers), turn off at the isolation switch. For HV vehicles, the supply normally disconnects by turning the vehicle off — but consider there may be a fault.

02. Separate Casualty from Source

If safe to do so, use a purpose-designed insulated rescue hook. The rescue hook should be kept visible and within easy reach at all times.

03. Perform Primary Survey

Once contact is broken, perform a primary survey and treat injuries. Seek assistance from the first aid person.

04. Call Emergency Services

Call 999 for emergency help immediately.

05. Secure the Scene

Cordon off the vehicle using a non-conductive barrier chain. No further work until risk assessed.

EV Charging Fundamentals

Understanding charging terminology and technology is essential for anyone working around electric vehicles.

Essential Charging Terms

Voltage (V)

The electrical pressure. ICE vehicles use 12V, trucks/buses 24V, Mild Hybrids 48V, Hybrids 200–400V, Full EVs 350–800V.

Amps (A)

Measurement of electrical flow — the rate at which charge moves through a circuit.

Watts (W) / Kilowatts (kW)

Energy consumption measurement. Charging speeds range from 2.3kW home charging to 360kW rapid chargers.

Charging Speeds Explained

01 / 03

Home Charging — 2.3kW

Standard household power (230V) × cable amperage (10A) = 2.3kW. A 52kWh battery takes approximately 22.6 hours at this rate.

02 / 03

Wall Box — 7.4kW to 22kW

Dedicated home or workplace wall boxes provide significantly faster charging. A 7.4kW wall box charges a 52kWh battery in around 7 hours.

03 / 03

Rapid Chargers — 50kW+

Public rapid chargers provide the fastest speeds. The UK's fastest deliver up to 360kW, adding significant range in minutes.

Glossary of EV Terms

Arc/Flash expand_more

A dangerous release of energy caused by an electrical arc between conductors. Can cause severe burns and ignite flammable materials.

BEV — Battery Electric Vehicle expand_more

A vehicle driven entirely by an electric motor powered by a rechargeable high voltage battery.

DC-DC Converter expand_more

Changes high voltage from the traction battery to low voltage for the vehicle's standard 12V systems.

ECE R100 expand_more

A UN regulation specifying tests for batteries in electric vehicles. Defines high voltage as >60V and ≤1500V DC.

FCEV — Fuel Cell Electric Vehicle expand_more

A vehicle powered by a fuel cell that converts hydrogen and oxygen into electricity.

HEV — Hybrid Electric Vehicle expand_more

A vehicle combining an internal combustion engine with an electric motor and smaller HV battery.

Insulated Gloves (Class 0) expand_more

Specialist safety gloves rated to 1500V DC, worn when working on high voltage systems.

PHEV — Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicle expand_more

A hybrid with a larger battery that can be charged from an external mains supply.

Power Electronics Controller expand_more

Contains the inverter and electronic controller that manages power flow in the vehicle.

Rescue Hook expand_more

An insulated tool used to safely separate a casualty from an electrical source during an emergency.

Traction Battery expand_more

The main high voltage battery (typically 60–800V) that powers the electric drive motors.
