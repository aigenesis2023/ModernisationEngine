Recognising an Electric Vehicle

Why Identification Matters

It is important that you can recognise an electric vehicle when it comes into your workplace. You will then be ready to exercise the correct procedures necessary for a safe visit.

Identifying features will differ between manufacturers. This means you may need to exercise your skill and experience to determine the vehicle type — getting it wrong could be dangerous.

Exterior Identification

Look for these key exterior features:

  • Number plate: Full EVs may have a green border (not mandatory)
  • Badging: Many EVs display a badge indicating electric power
  • Charge port: BEVs and PHEVs have a battery charge connector port on the vehicle exterior

Under the Bonnet

When inspecting the engine bay, look for these indicators:

  • Orange high-voltage cables — the universal indicator of an EV electrical system
  • Power controller / inverter — converts battery DC to motor AC
  • Warning labels — high-voltage warnings on components and covers

EV Identification Checklist

  • check_circle

    Badging on exterior bodywork

    Look for EV, Electric, e-tron, or similar manufacturer badges.

  • check_circle

    External mains charging connection port

    Present on BEV and PHEV vehicles — not on standard HEV.

  • check_circle

    Instrument cluster warnings and messages

    Dashboard displays showing EV status and high-voltage warnings.

  • check_circle

    Power flow / energy use screen on dashboard

    Shows real-time energy flow between battery, motor, and wheels.

  • check_circle

    Bright-coloured (orange) high-voltage cables

    The universal indicator of high-voltage systems in any EV.

  • check_circle

    Green band on number plate

    Not mandatory — do not rely on this alone.

  • check_circle

    Warning labels on HV components

    Yellow and black high-voltage warning triangles on covers and components.

Not all types of EV will have the same identifying features. A Hybrid Electric Vehicle (HEV) may not have a charging port, but it still contains high-voltage components that present serious risks.

Understanding the Hazards

High-voltage systems in EVs present unique risks that require specific knowledge and precautions.

Hazards & Legislation

High-Voltage Hazards in the Workplace

Electric and hybrid vehicles introduce hazards into the workplace in addition to those normally associated with vehicle repair and maintenance.

Voltages present in EVs are significantly higher — currently up to 650 Volts DC — compared to conventional vehicles (12/24V DC). In dry conditions, accidental contact with parts live at voltages above 110V DC can be fatal.

Battery systems may contain chemicals that can be harmful if released. They also store significant amounts of energy that can give rise to explosion if not dealt with correctly.

Key Risks of Working with EVs

Fatal Electric Shock expand_more

The presence of high-voltage components and cabling capable of delivering a fatal electric shock. Components may retain dangerous voltage even when the vehicle is switched off.

Fire & Explosion Risk expand_more

The storage of electrical energy with the potential to cause explosion or fire. Stored electrical energy can be sufficient to cause arc flash — a sudden release of energy through the air.

Silent Operation expand_more

The possibility of people being unaware of vehicles moving, as when electrically driven they are silent in operation. Most EVs have AVAS (Acoustic Vehicle Alerting System), but the sound may not be what you expect.

Unexpected Movement expand_more

Electric motors or the vehicle itself may move unexpectedly due to magnetic forces within the motors. Vehicles can be ready to drive without a running engine.

Battery Chemical Hazards expand_more

The potential for release of explosive gases and harmful liquids and chemicals if batteries are damaged or incorrectly modified. Manual handling risks are also associated with battery replacement.

Medical Device Interference expand_more

The potential for the electrical systems on the vehicle to affect medical devices such as pacemakers. Personnel with medical implants should exercise additional caution.

HSE Work Categories

The UK HSE identifies 4 categories of EV work: valeting/sales (low risk), incident response, routine maintenance (excluding HV), and working on high-voltage electrical systems (specialist only).

Low-Risk Activity Precautions

Even for valeting, sales, and other lower-risk activities around EVs, these precautions must be followed:

Step 01

Secure the Key Fob

Keep remote operation keys away from the vehicle to prevent accidental power-up. Use a signal-blocking pouch if available.

Step 02

Be Aware of Silent Movement

People moving EVs around the workplace should be aware that others may not hear them approaching. Sound your horn when necessary.

Step 03

Avoid Pressure Washing HV Components

High-pressure water can damage high-voltage electrical components and cables. Orange cables indicate HV systems — refer to manufacturer guidance before valeting.

Step 04

Never Touch Orange Cables

Orange cables carry high voltage. Never handle, move, or lean tools against them. Report any visible damage immediately.

Electric Shock & Emergency Response

What Is an Electric Shock?

A voltage as low as 50 volts applied between two parts of the human body causes a current to flow that can block the electrical signals between the brain and the muscles.

This may cause:

  • Stopping the heart beating properly
  • Preventing the person from breathing
  • Causing muscle spasms
  • Secondary injuries such as burns and fall injuries

Shock Severity Factors

The severity of an electric shock depends on a combination of factors:

01 / 04

Voltage Level

EV systems operate at up to 650V DC — far exceeding the 50V threshold for danger. Higher voltage drives more current through the body.

02 / 04

Body Resistance

Dry skin offers more resistance than wet skin. Non-conductive materials reduce current flow, while high-conductivity materials increase it significantly.

03 / 04

Current Path

Finger-to-finger (same hand) is less dangerous than hand-to-foot or across the chest, which can pass through the heart.

04 / 04

Duration of Contact

Even milliseconds of contact can cause injury. One second or more of sustained contact with high voltage is frequently fatal.

1 / 4

Emergency Response Procedure

If you encounter someone receiving an electric shock, follow these steps in order:

  1. 01

    Assess the Situation

    Do not touch the casualty if they are still in contact with the electrical source — you are at risk of electrocution.

  2. 02

    Isolate the Power Source

    For mains chargers, turn off power at the isolation switch. For vehicles, the HV supply should disconnect when turning off with the key — but consider there may be a fault.

  3. 03

    Separate Casualty from Source

    Use a purpose-designed insulated rescue hook. Do not attempt untested rescue methods if you are not trained.

  4. 04

    Call 999

    Call emergency services immediately. Specify this is a high-voltage electrical incident.

  5. 05

    Provide First Aid

    Once contact is broken, perform a primary survey and treat injuries. Seek assistance from qualified first aid personnel.

  6. 06

    Secure the Scene

    Ensure no one else works on the vehicle until made safe. Cordon off with non-conductive barrier chain.

Safety Equipment

Essential EV Safety Equipment

When electric vehicles enter your workplace, specific safety equipment must be available and correctly used. Test your knowledge of each piece of equipment below.

Equipment Overview

Vehicle ID Sign

Should be present on every electric vehicle entering a workshop for repair so that all staff are aware it is an EV.

Barrier Chain

A non-conductive safety barrier used to alert others and prevent unauthorised access to the vehicle work area.

Key Signal Blocker

A protective pouch that blocks wireless signals from car keys, preventing accidental power-up on keyless-start vehicles.

Rescue Hook

An insulated rescue hook for trained staff to remove a casualty from an electric shock source. Must be kept visible and within easy reach.

Insulated Gloves

Class 0 rated gloves for up to 1500V DC. For suitably trained persons working on HV systems only.

Warning Signs

Warning and status signs that alert others to risks present. Includes 'Do Not Start / Work in Progress' signs.

Risk Assessment

Matching Risks to Systems

Understanding which risks relate to which vehicle systems is critical for safe working.

  • Ready to drive without running engine: Risk of unexpected movement
  • Silent electric motor operation: Pedestrians may not notice moving vehicles
  • Automatic stop-start systems: Vehicle may start unexpectedly
  • High-voltage components: Risk of electric shock from battery, cables, generator

Risk Mitigation Strategies

Charging Terminology

Understanding Charging

To work safely around EV charging systems, you need to understand the basic electrical terminology. These concepts determine charging speed, cable ratings, and safety thresholds.

Key Electrical Terms

bolt
What are Watts?

Watts measure energy consumption — the rate at which electrical energy is used or generated. EV chargers are rated in kilowatts (kW).

speed
What are Amps?

Amps (amperes) measure electrical current — the flow of electricity through a circuit. A higher amp reading means more current is flowing.

power
What is Voltage?

Voltage is electrical pressure — the force that pushes current through a circuit. ICE vehicles use 12V, trucks 24V, but EVs operate at 350-800V DC.

memory
What does kWh mean?

Kilowatt-hours measure battery capacity. A 52kWh battery charging at 2.3kW takes about 22.6 hours. Typical EV batteries range from 40-100kWh.
