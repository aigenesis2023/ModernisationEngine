Recognising an Electric Vehicle

Why Identification Matters

It is critical that you can recognise an electric vehicle when it enters your workplace. Identifying features will differ between manufacturers, so you may need to exercise your skill and experience to determine the vehicle type — getting it wrong could be dangerous.

Exterior Identification

Look for these key exterior features:

  • Number plate: Full EVs may have a green border (not mandatory)
  • Badging: Many EVs display a badge indicating electric power
  • Charge port: BEVs and PHEVs have a battery charge connector port on the vehicle exterior

Under the Bonnet

When inspecting the engine bay, look for these indicators:

  • Orange high-voltage cables — the universal indicator of an EV electrical system
  • Power controller / inverter — converts battery DC to motor AC
  • Warning labels — high-voltage warnings on components and covers

EV Identification Checklist

  • check_circle

    Badging on exterior bodywork

    Look for EV, Electric, e-tron, or similar manufacturer badges.

  • check_circle

    External mains charging connection port

  • check_circle

  • check_circle

  • check_circle

  • check_circle

    Not mandatory — do not rely on this alone.

  • check_circle

    Warning labels on HV components

Not all types of EV will have the same identifying features. A Hybrid Electric Vehicle (HEV) may not have a charging port, but it still contains high-voltage components that present serious risks.

Understanding the Hazards

High-voltage systems in EVs present unique risks that require specific knowledge and precautions.

Hazards & Legislation

High-Voltage Hazards in the Workplace

Electric and hybrid vehicles introduce hazards into the workplace in addition to those normally associated with vehicle repair and maintenance. Understanding these risks is the first step to working safely.

Key Risks of Working with EVs

Fatal Electric Shock expand_more

The presence of high-voltage components and cabling capable of delivering a fatal electric shock. Voltages in EVs can reach up to 650V DC — significantly higher than the 12/24V found in conventional vehicles. Components may retain dangerous voltage even when the vehicle is switched off.

Fire & Explosion Risk expand_more

The storage of electrical energy with the potential to cause explosion or fire. Stored electrical energy can be released suddenly if batteries are damaged or incorrectly modified, releasing explosive gases and harmful chemicals.

Silent Operation expand_more

Electric motors operate silently, meaning people may be unaware of vehicles moving. Most EVs are fitted with a pedestrian sound emitter, but this may not always be active in workshop environments.

Unexpected Movement expand_more

Electric motors can generate magnetic forces that cause unexpected movement. Remote operation keys that only need to be near the vehicle can accidentally power it up.

Medical Device Interference expand_more

High-voltage electrical systems may affect medical devices such as pacemakers. Staff with such devices should be made aware of this risk.

Battery Handling expand_more

Manual handling risks associated with battery replacement. Batteries are extremely heavy. Hybrid vehicles also retain a conventional fuel tank, adding further complexity.

ECE R100 Certification

A UN regulation that defines 'High Voltage' as >60V to ≤1500V DC (or >30V to ≤1000V AC). This is the standard used when referring to electric vehicle high-voltage systems.

power
HSE Work Categories

The UK HSE identifies 4 categories of EV work: valeting/sales (low risk), incident response, routine maintenance, and high-voltage system work (specialist only).

Low-Risk Activity Precautions

Even for valeting, sales, and other lower-risk activities around EVs, these precautions must be followed:

Step 01

Secure the Key Fob

Keep remote operation keys away from the vehicle to prevent accidental power-up. Use a signal-blocking pouch if available.

Step 02

Be Aware of Silent Movement

People moving EVs around the workplace should be aware that others may not hear them approaching. Sound your horn when necessary.

Step 03

Avoid Pressure Washing HV Components

High-pressure water can damage high-voltage electrical components and cables. Orange cables indicate HV systems — refer to manufacturer guidance before valeting in these areas.

Step 04

Never Touch Orange Cables

Orange cables carry high voltage. Never handle, move, or lean tools against them. Report any visible damage immediately.

Electric Shock & Emergency Response

What Is an Electric Shock?

A voltage as low as 50 volts applied between two parts of the human body causes a current to flow that can block the electrical signals between the brain and the muscles.

This may cause:

  • Stopping the heart beating properly
  • Preventing the person from breathing
  • Causing muscle spasms
  • Secondary injuries such as burns and fall injuries

Shock Severity Factors

The severity of an electric shock depends on a combination of factors:

01 / 04

Voltage Level

EV systems operate at up to 650V DC — far exceeding the 50V threshold for danger. Higher voltage drives more current through the body.

02 / 04

Current Path Through Body

The path the current takes matters greatly. Hand-to-hand across the chest is the most dangerous. Finger-to-finger on the same hand is less severe but still harmful.

03 / 04

Duration of Contact

Even fractions of a second of contact with high voltage can be fatal. Muscle spasms may prevent the person from releasing their grip, prolonging exposure.

04 / 04

Skin Conductivity

Wet or sweaty skin dramatically reduces resistance, allowing more current to flow. Always ensure dry conditions when near HV components.

1 / 4

Emergency Response Procedure

If you encounter someone receiving an electric shock, follow these steps in order:

  1. 01

    Assess the Situation

    Do NOT touch the casualty if they are still in contact with the electrical source. You are at risk of electrocution.

  2. 02

    Isolate the Power Source

    For mains chargers, turn off power at the isolation switch. For vehicle HV systems, turn off the vehicle using the normal key. Consider that the vehicle may not be safe even when switched off.

  3. 03

    Separate Casualty from Source

    If safe to do so, use a purpose-designed insulated rescue hook to separate the casualty from the power supply. Never use bare hands or conductive materials.

  4. 04

    Call 999

    Call emergency services immediately. Provide details about the high-voltage nature of the incident.

  5. 05

    Provide First Aid

    Once contact is broken, perform a primary survey and treat injuries. Seek assistance from trained first aiders on site.

  6. 06

    Secure the Scene

    Cordon off the vehicle using a non-conductive safety barrier chain. Ensure no one else attempts to work on it until it has been made safe by a specialist.

Safety Equipment

Essential EV Safety Equipment

When electric vehicles enter your workplace, specific safety equipment must be available and correctly used. The following items should be present in any workshop or facility that handles EVs.

Equipment Guide

Vehicle ID Sign

Should be present on every electric vehicle entering a workshop for repair so that all staff are aware it is an EV. This is the primary visual indicator that special precautions apply.

Barrier Chain

A non-conductive safety barrier chain or cordon that alerts others and prevents unauthorised access to the EV work area. Must be non-conductive to prevent creating an additional electrical path.

Key Signal Blocker

A pouch or case that prevents accidental power-up on keyless-start vehicles by blocking the key from receiving and sending signals to the vehicle. Essential for preventing unexpected movement.

Rescue Hook

An insulated hook to be used by suitably trained staff in an emergency to remove a casualty from the source of an electric shock. Must be rated for the voltages present and regularly inspected.

Insulated Gloves

Class 0 insulated gloves rated to 1500V DC. Should be worn by suitably trained persons when working on the high-voltage system. Must be inspected before each use for tears or damage.

Warning Signs

Signs that alert others to the risks present in the EV work area. Includes 'Do Not Start / Work in Progress' signs to prevent unauthorised starting during repair.

Charging Terminology

Understanding Charging

To work safely around EV charging systems, you need to understand the basic electrical terminology. These concepts determine charging speed, cable ratings, and safety thresholds.

Key Electrical Terms

bolt
What are Watts?

Watts measure energy consumption — the rate at which electrical energy is used or generated. We're familiar with this from household items like lightbulbs. EV chargers are rated in kilowatts (kW).

speed
What are Amps?

Amps (amperes) measure electrical current — the flow of electricity through a circuit. There is a relationship between voltage, amps, and resistance. A higher amp reading means more current is flowing.

power
What is Voltage?

Voltage is electrical pressure — the force that pushes current through a circuit. ICE vehicles use 12V, trucks use 24V, but EVs operate at 400-650V DC. This is why EV electrical systems are so dangerous.

memory
What does kWh mean?

Kilowatt-hours measure battery capacity — how much energy the battery can store. A larger kWh rating means greater range. Typical EV batteries range from 40kWh to 100kWh.
