### SECTION: Types of Electric Vehicle

[FULL-BLEED] Understanding the EV Family
(Full-width parallax image with text overlay)
<p>Electric vehicles come in many forms — from mild hybrids to fully electric and hydrogen fuel cell. Each has different components, capabilities, and safety considerations.</p>

[TABS] EV Types Compared
INTERACTIVE: Add data-tabs on container, data-tab-trigger on buttons, data-tab-panel on panels.
<p>Click each tab to learn about the different types of electric vehicle.</p>
  Tab "BEV — Battery Electric": <p>Driven 100% by electric motor with a large capacity rechargeable high voltage battery.</p><p><strong>Advantages:</strong> No tailpipe emissions, greatest electric-only driving range of all EV types. Easy to drive.</p><p><strong>Disadvantages:</strong> Reliance on charging network, battery repair costs compared to ICE repair costs.</p>
  Tab "HEV — Hybrid Electric": <p>Internal combustion engine combined with electric motor and smaller high voltage battery capacity.</p><p><strong>Advantages:</strong> Good compromise between range and clean running. Optimised use of both powertrains can lead to very high MPG figures.</p><p><strong>Disadvantages:</strong> Extra cost of EV systems. Battery-only range will be less than a BEV due to smaller battery sizes.</p>
  Tab "PHEV — Plug-in Hybrid": <p>High voltage battery can be charged by an external mains charger, hence the "Plug-in" hybrid name.</p><p><strong>Advantages:</strong> Offers both range and clean running. Larger battery than non plug-in hybrids. Can be charged from the mains.</p><p><strong>Disadvantages:</strong> Cost of EV systems means these vehicles are more expensive. Battery-only range will still be less than a BEV.</p>
  Tab "EREV — Extended-Range": <p>100% electric motor drive. An external fuel-based power unit drives a generator to charge the battery and extend the vehicle range.</p><p><strong>Advantages:</strong> Combines electric drive with extended range from a fuel-powered generator.</p><p><strong>Disadvantages:</strong> Stretches the definition of "Hybrid". Complex dual powertrain.</p>
  Tab "FCEV — Fuel Cell Electric": <p>Driven 100% by electric motor. Fuel cell converts hydrogen and oxygen into electric power.</p><p><strong>Advantages:</strong> Very clean running. Long range. Zero tailpipe emissions, producing only clean water vapour.</p><p><strong>Disadvantages:</strong> Expensive and rare. Hydrogen fueling infrastructure is not widely available.</p>
  Tab "ICE — Conventional": <p>Traditional internal combustion engine running on petrol or diesel.</p><p><strong>Advantages:</strong> No cost of batteries or EV HV control systems. Good range. Quick to refuel.</p><p><strong>Disadvantages:</strong> Tailpipe emissions, noise pollution. Being phased out in various countries.</p>

[GRAPHIC-TEXT] Inside a Fully Electric Vehicle
<p>A fully electric vehicle contains several key high voltage components:</p><ul><li><strong>HV Battery Pack:</strong> Voltage approx. 60–800V, powers the electric drive motors</li><li><strong>Power Electronics Controller:</strong> Contains the inverter to change AC to DC current and controls power flow</li><li><strong>Electric Drive Motor:</strong> Powered by high voltage AC to drive the vehicle</li><li><strong>On-board Charger:</strong> Takes mains electricity and charges the vehicle's HV battery</li><li><strong>DC-DC Converter:</strong> Changes high voltage to low voltage for vehicle systems</li><li><strong>Battery Cooling System:</strong> Keeps the battery at optimal temperature</li></ul>

### SECTION: EV Safety & Hazards

[FULL-BLEED] Hazards Around High Voltage Electrical Systems
(Full-width parallax image with text overlay)
<p>Understanding the dangers associated with electric and hybrid vehicles is critical to keeping yourself and your colleagues safe.</p>

[TEXT] HSE Legislation
<p>The UK Health and Safety Executive states that people in the motor vehicle repair and recovery industry need to be aware of the additional hazards when working with Electric & Hybrid Vehicles.</p><p><strong>Key fact:</strong> Voltages in E&HVs reach up to 650 Volts DC — far higher than the 12/24V in conventional vehicles. In dry conditions, contact with parts live at voltages above 110V DC can be fatal.</p><p>Battery systems may contain harmful chemicals and store significant energy that can cause explosion if not handled correctly.</p>

[ACCORDION] Workplace Risks from Electric & Hybrid Vehicles

  • Electric Shock & Arc Flash: <p>High voltage components and cabling capable of delivering a fatal electric shock. Stored electrical energy can cause explosion, fire, and arc/flash.</p>

[GRAPHIC-TEXT] What Is an Electric Shock?
<p>A voltage as low as 50 volts can block electrical signals between the brain and muscles, causing:</p><ul><li>Stopping the heart beating properly</li><li>Preventing the person from breathing</li><li>Causing muscle spasms</li><li>Secondary injuries such as burns and fall injuries</li></ul>
(Side-by-side text + image layout. Include a relevant image.)

[TIMELINE] Emergency Response — Electric Shock
<p>If you encounter someone receiving an electric shock, follow these steps.</p>
  Step 01: Isolate the Power — <p>For mains systems (chargers), turn off at the isolation switch. For HV vehicles, the supply normally disconnects by turning the vehicle off — but consider there may be a fault.</p>
  Step 02: Separate Casualty from Source — <p>If safe to do so, use a purpose-designed insulated rescue hook. The rescue hook should be kept visible and within easy reach at all times.</p>
  Step 03: Perform Primary Survey — <p>Once contact is broken, perform a primary survey and treat injuries. Seek assistance from the first aid person.</p>
  Step 04: Call Emergency Services — <p>Call 999 for emergency help immediately.</p>
  Step 05: Secure the Scene — <p>Cordon off the vehicle using a non-conductive barrier chain. No further work until risk assessed.</p>

[GRAPHIC-TEXT] Severity of Electric Shock
<p>The severity of an electric shock is determined by four factors:</p><ul><li><strong>Voltage:</strong> Low, medium, or high</li><li><strong>Resistance:</strong> Non-conductive, low-conductivity, or high-conductivity material</li><li><strong>Path:</strong> Finger to finger (same hand), hand to foot, or across the body</li><li><strong>Duration:</strong> Milliseconds, fractions of a second, or one second or more</li></ul><p>A high voltage through high-conductivity material across the body for more than a second is the most dangerous combination.</p>

### SECTION: Charging

[FULL-BLEED] EV Charging Fundamentals
(Full-width parallax image with text overlay)
<p>Understanding charging terminology and technology is essential for anyone working around electric vehicles.</p>

[KEY-TERM] Essential Charging Terms
  Voltage (V): The electrical pressure. ICE vehicles use 12V, trucks/buses 24V, Mild Hybrids 48V, Hybrids 200–400V, Full EVs 350–800V.
  Amps (A): Measurement of electrical flow — the rate at which charge moves through a circuit.
  Watts (W) / Kilowatts (kW): Energy consumption measurement. Charging speeds range from 2.3kW home charging to 360kW rapid chargers.

### SECTION: Glossary & Course Completion

[ACCORDION] Glossary of EV Terms
Instruction: Select each item to learn more.
INTERACTIVE: Use native <details><summary> elements.
  • Arc/Flash: <p>A dangerous release of energy caused by an electrical arc between conductors. Can cause severe burns and ignite flammable materials.</p>
  • BEV — Battery Electric Vehicle: <p>A vehicle driven entirely by an electric motor powered by a rechargeable high voltage battery.</p>
  • DC-DC Converter: <p>Changes high voltage from the traction battery to low voltage for the vehicle's standard 12V systems.</p>
  • ECE R100: <p>A UN regulation specifying tests for batteries in electric vehicles. Defines high voltage as >60V and ≤1500V DC.</p>
  • FCEV — Fuel Cell Electric Vehicle: <p>A vehicle powered by a fuel cell that converts hydrogen and oxygen into electricity.</p>
  • HEV — Hybrid Electric Vehicle: <p>A vehicle combining an internal combustion engine with an electric motor and smaller HV battery.</p>
  • Insulated Gloves (Class 0): <p>Specialist safety gloves rated to 1500V DC, worn when working on high voltage systems.</p>
  • PHEV — Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicle: <p>A hybrid with a larger battery that can be charged from an external mains supply.</p>
  • Power Electronics Controller: <p>Contains the inverter and electronic controller that manages power flow in the vehicle.</p>
  • Rescue Hook: <p>An insulated tool used to safely separate a casualty from an electrical source during an emergency.</p>
  • Traction Battery: <p>The main high voltage battery (typically 60–800V) that powers the electric drive motors.</p>
