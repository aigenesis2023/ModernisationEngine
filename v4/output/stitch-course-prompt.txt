### SECTION: Hazards & Legislation

[TEXT] High-Voltage Hazards in the Workplace
<p>Electric and hybrid vehicles introduce hazards into the workplace in addition to those normally associated with vehicle repair and maintenance. Understanding these risks is the first step to working safely.</p>

[ACCORDION] Key Risks of Working with EVs
Instruction: Select each risk category to learn more.
INTERACTIVE: Use native <details><summary> elements.
  • Fatal Electric Shock: <p>The presence of high-voltage components and cabling capable of delivering a fatal electric shock. Voltages in EVs can reach up to 650V DC — significantly higher than the 12/24V found in conventional vehicles. Components may retain dangerous voltage even when the vehicle is switched off.</p>
  • Fire & Explosion Risk: <p>The storage of electrical energy with the potential to cause explosion or fire. Stored electrical energy can be released suddenly if batteries are damaged or incorrectly modified, releasing explosive gases and harmful chemicals.</p>
  • Silent Operation: <p>Electric motors operate silently, meaning people may be unaware of vehicles moving. Most EVs are fitted with a pedestrian sound emitter, but this may not always be active in workshop environments.</p>
  • Unexpected Movement: <p>Electric motors can generate magnetic forces that cause unexpected movement. Remote operation keys that only need to be near the vehicle can accidentally power it up.</p>
  • Medical Device Interference: <p>High-voltage electrical systems may affect medical devices such as pacemakers. Staff with such devices should be made aware of this risk.</p>
  • Battery Handling: <p>Manual handling risks associated with battery replacement. Batteries are extremely heavy. Hybrid vehicles also retain a conventional fuel tank, adding further complexity.</p>

[KEY-TERM] Legislation & Standards
  HSE Guidelines: The UK Health and Safety Executive provides specific guidance for working with electric and hybrid vehicles in the motor vehicle repair industry. Source: hse.gov.uk/mvr/topics/electric-hybrid.htm
  ECE R100 Certification: A UN regulation that defines 'High Voltage' as >60V to ≤1500V DC (or >30V to ≤1000V AC). This is the standard used when referring to electric vehicle high-voltage systems.
  HSE Work Categories: The UK HSE identifies 4 categories of EV work: valeting/sales (low risk), incident response, routine maintenance, and high-voltage system work (specialist only).

[TIMELINE] Low-Risk Activity Precautions
<p>Even for valeting, sales, and other lower-risk activities around EVs, these precautions must be followed:</p>
  Step 01: Secure the Key Fob — <p>Keep remote operation keys away from the vehicle to prevent accidental power-up. Use a signal-blocking pouch if available.</p>
  Step 02: Be Aware of Silent Movement — <p>People moving EVs around the workplace should be aware that others may not hear them approaching. Sound your horn when necessary.</p>
  Step 03: Avoid Pressure Washing HV Components — <p>High-pressure water can damage high-voltage electrical components and cables. Orange cables indicate HV systems — refer to manufacturer guidance before valeting in these areas.</p>
  Step 04: Never Touch Orange Cables — <p>Orange cables carry high voltage. Never handle, move, or lean tools against them. Report any visible damage immediately.</p>

### SECTION: Electric Shock & Emergency Response

[GRAPHIC-TEXT] What Is an Electric Shock?
<p>A voltage as low as <strong>50 volts</strong> applied between two parts of the human body causes a current to flow that can block the electrical signals between the brain and the muscles.</p><p>This may cause:</p><ul><li>Stopping the heart beating properly</li><li>Preventing the person from breathing</li><li>Causing muscle spasms</li><li>Secondary injuries such as burns and fall injuries</li></ul>
(Side-by-side text + image layout. Include a relevant image.)

[NARRATIVE] Shock Severity Factors
INTERACTIVE: Add data-carousel, data-slide, data-prev, data-next.
<p>The severity of an electric shock depends on a combination of factors:</p>
  Slide: Voltage Level — <p>EV systems operate at up to 650V DC — far exceeding the 50V threshold for danger. Higher voltage drives more current through the body.</p>
  Slide: Current Path Through Body — <p>The path the current takes matters greatly. Hand-to-hand across the chest is the most dangerous. Finger-to-finger on the same hand is less severe but still harmful.</p>
  Slide: Duration of Contact — <p>Even fractions of a second of contact with high voltage can be fatal. Muscle spasms may prevent the person from releasing their grip, prolonging exposure.</p>
  Slide: Skin Conductivity — <p>Wet or sweaty skin dramatically reduces resistance, allowing more current to flow. Always ensure dry conditions when near HV components.</p>

[PROCESS-FLOW] Emergency Response Procedure
<p>If you encounter someone receiving an electric shock, follow these steps in order:</p>
  → Assess the Situation: Do NOT touch the casualty if they are still in contact with the electrical source. You are at risk of electrocution.
  → Isolate the Power Source: For mains chargers, turn off power at the isolation switch. For vehicle HV systems, turn off the vehicle using the normal key. Consider that the vehicle may not be safe even when switched off.
  → Separate Casualty from Source: If safe to do so, use a purpose-designed insulated rescue hook to separate the casualty from the power supply. Never use bare hands or conductive materials.
  → Call 999: Call emergency services immediately. Provide details about the high-voltage nature of the incident.
  → Provide First Aid: Once contact is broken, perform a primary survey and treat injuries. Seek assistance from trained first aiders on site.
  → Secure the Scene: Cordon off the vehicle using a non-conductive safety barrier chain. Ensure no one else attempts to work on it until it has been made safe by a specialist.

### SECTION: Charging Terminology

[FLASHCARD] Key Electrical Terms
INTERACTIVE: Add data-flashcard on each card (click to flip).
Click each card to reveal the definition.
  Front: What are Watts? → Back: Watts measure energy consumption — the rate at which electrical energy is used or generated. We're familiar with this from household items like lightbulbs. EV chargers are rated in kilowatts (kW).
  Front: What are Amps? → Back: Amps (amperes) measure electrical current — the flow of electricity through a circuit. There is a relationship between voltage, amps, and resistance. A higher amp reading means more current is flowing.
  Front: What is Voltage? → Back: Voltage is electrical pressure — the force that pushes current through a circuit. ICE vehicles use 12V, trucks use 24V, but EVs operate at 400-650V DC. This is why EV electrical systems are so dangerous.
  Front: What does kWh mean? → Back: Kilowatt-hours measure battery capacity — how much energy the battery can store. A larger kWh rating means greater range. Typical EV batteries range from 40kWh to 100kWh.

### SECTION: Glossary & Summary

[KEY-TERM] Key Terms Reference
  Arc Flash: A sudden release of electrical energy through the air when a high-voltage gap is breached. Can cause serious burns, injuries, or equipment damage.
  BEV: Battery Electric Vehicle — powered entirely by a rechargeable battery pack with no internal combustion engine.
  HEV: Hybrid Electric Vehicle — combines a conventional engine with an electric motor. Battery charges through regenerative braking only.
  PHEV: Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicle — has both a combustion engine and electric motor with external charging capability.
  ECE R100: The UN regulation defining high voltage as >60V to ≤1500V DC for electric vehicles. Specifies all required safety tests for EV approval.
